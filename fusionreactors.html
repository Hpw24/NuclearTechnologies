Fusion Reactors


Fusion reactors are still being researched. There are currently operational reactors, but they could never be used as powerplants at the moment. This is because they require more energy to operate than the give out but scientist are hopeful that they can make them more efficient so we can start benefiting from them. The main reason they are so inefficient is that the process needs temperature in excess of 100 million degrees centigrade.

Current Design Ideas:

Inertial Confinement:

In this design a pellet of fuel about the size of a pea is compressed by lasers or ion beams to an incredibly high density. Once at this high density the pellet is then ignited using shock wave heating. This process is repeated multiple times per second to create enough energy to boil water and drive a turbine like a conventional power plant.

Magnetic Confinement:

Powerful electromagnets use magnetic fields to contain plasma fuel. Then a large current of electricity is induced in the plasma causing heating so that the fuel can undergo fusion. Usually there are other methods such as microwaves which are used to further heat the fuel so it can undergo fusion. The heat would once again be used to drive a turbine.


Fuel:

The fuel used in both reactors would be a combination of Deuterium and Tritium which are both isotope of hydrogen. Deuterium can be found in small quantities naturally in sea water which it can rather economically be extracted from while Tritium can be made from the common element Lithium. It has been estimated that in one litre of seawater contains enough Deuterium and therefor the potential to be turned into energy as 300 litres of oil undergoing combustion. This is why a fusion reactor would have a near limitless supply of water due to the Earths huge oceans.
